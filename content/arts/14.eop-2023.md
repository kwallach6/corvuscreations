# Evening of Performance 2023

![The EoP 2023 cast photo](/eop-2023/20230218-184320.jpg)

The 2023 Evening of Performance was my first lighting design for the RPI Players.  Everything was going smoothly and looking great, until February 4, 2023 when the RPI Playhouse flooded on the first day of tech week.  In a matter of days, all the work I did to get this design together had to be adapted for a different venue before the show opened in less than a week.

## Original Lighting Design

The show chosen for this Evening of Performance, in addition to the performances by outside groups, was a student-written adaptation of various Shakespeare plays, titled *Shakespeare Fight Club*.  This show presents a reenactment of Shakespeare's most famous duels in a format similar to a gameshow.  As such, my central design theme was to make fun of the way sports arenas are lit: cold white washes that eliminate all shadows.  Another theme was a red wash whenever someone dies, as is common in video games.  I also planned for a followspot for both duelists, as well as movers to mimic the audience blinders used in sports events.  Check out my original design notes, as well as my original cue list and light plot below!

## The Playhouse Flood

![The flooded RPI Playhouse](/eop-2023/flood.jpg)

On February 4, 2023, the RPI Playhouse flooded around 6 PM, just hours after the first tech rehearsals had finished.  All of my cues had been programmed, all of the lights had been focused, and a successful tech run had finished.  This was the one and only time I ever saw my original design realized, and unfortunately, there are no pictures of it.

::accordion{type="single"}
  :::accordion-item{label="The Playhouse Flood Story"}
  The RPI Playhouse flood was no accident, but nobody expected it to happen. The RPI Playhouse is one of, if not the oldest building on RPI's campus. It was built in the mid 1800s in Connecticut and moved to Troy, NY in the early 1900s to become RPI's 15th St Lounge. In the mid 1900s, it was renovated into the RPI Playhouse, a home for the RPI Players to produce their shows. Then, in 1980, its most recent renovation was completed. In the years leading up to the flood, the building began having several problems, including a malfunctioning HVAC system, electrical problems, and a near-failing of the most recent rigging system inspection, to name a few. The Rensselaer Union had been planning a renovation of the building to convert the lobby into a secondary performance space and build a new lobby, conference room, and box office on the north side of the building.

  On February 3, 2023, the RPI Players had their typical Friday night "Work Party" in the Playhouse. Nothing appeared amiss at the time, but overnight, the outside temperature dropped below -20 degrees Fahrenheit. On the morning of February 4, 2023, I arrived at the Playhouse at 7:30 AM to continue the lighting focus, but stood out in the cold waiting to be let in for a half hour. Once inside, we discovered that it was freezing and the heat was not on. The Technical Committee checked the boiler room and found that the RPI Facilities department had inexplicably shut off the boiler and made no mention of it. The Technical Director turned on the boiler and called the RPI Public Safety office to report the problem, but did not get an answer. Rehearsal proceeded as planned despite the cold, but there was no indication of any larger problems.

  Later that day, rehearsal was interrupted by a leak at the water fountain in the lobby. The Technical Director shut off the water main and again called Public Safety, and this time, officers arrived to inspect the building. Without any explanation, they turned the water back on and plugged the fountain. Rehearsal then resumed without incident.

  Following the water fountain incident, rehearsal was interrupted again by a loud bang coming from the lobby. No water had leaked, but the cast and crew observed that two fire sprinkler heads had fallen through the ceiling. The Technical Director roped off the area and called Public Safety again, but got no answer. By this point, the heat was on and the building was beginning to warm. Rehearsal resumed for the rest of the day and ended around 4 PM.

  Around 2 hours after rehearsal ended and everyone left the building, someone sent a message on the RPI Players Discord server that included a video of the fire alarm sounding and emergency personnel responding. When the Technical Committee arrived, they discovered that the building had flooded from water gushing out of the fallen sprinkler pipes. They concluded that the falling sprinklers were a result of the pipes bursting, and when the pipes thawed, water rushed out. The water was knee-deep in the area of the burst pipe and destroyed the lobby, props closet, bathrooms, concessions stand, and house floor. Miraculously, no props or equipment was damaged, and nobody was injured, as the building was vacant when the flood began.

  Following the flood, the building was closed indefinitely. Over the next 2 years, the Technical Committee, Executive Committee, and the Rensselaer Union negotiated repairs and renovations. The Union had suggested completing the renovations instead of just making repairs, as the walls would already be open. Eventually, the renovation plan was scrapped in favor of making the repairs and bringing the building up to code. At this point, the Union was arguing that the Facilities department should pay for the repairs, as they were all safety-related. Meanwhile, the Facilities department argued that the Union should pay, as the Playhouse is a student activity building. Ultimately, this argument went nowhere and, meanwhile, the repair costs ballooned to over $5 million. As of now, due to this high cost, the Playhouse will likely be demolished in favor of expanding a nearby research facility. Meanwhile, the RPI Players continue to be on the road with an ever-shrinking budget and ballooning operating costs.
  :::

## 4 Plots In 4 Days

Immediately after the flood, the anticipated path to ensuring the show continued was partnering with Union Show Techs to perform in the Rensselaer Union McNeil Room.  I immediately drew a new lighting plot for this scenario, making use of whatever equipment was retrieved from the Playhouse immediately after the flood.  My goal was to not change my original ideas while adapting them to fit a new space.  The plot below was my idea for the McNeil room but, fortunately, it became irrelevant.

![My lighting plot for the McNeil Room](/eop-2023/EoP-McNeil-Plot.png)

Days later, EMPAC offered to host the show in Studio Beta, a rehearsal studio that is intended for student club use.  After hearing the news, I drew another plot that made use of the equipment from the Playhouse.  The problem, however, is that Studio Beta does not have a dimming system, which would have made powering this equipment difficult or impossible.

![My incandescent lighting plot for Studio Beta](/eop-2023/EoP-EMPAC-Plot.jpg)

Aware of the power problem, EMPAC offered 10 of their ETC Source 4 LED Lustr 2 fixtures for us to use.  This eliminated the power constrains entirely and reduced the number of fixtures needed to realize my design.  Once aware of this offer, I drew a final lighting plot and adjusted my cue list to match it.

## The Show Goes On

![The first duel in Studio Beta](/eop-2023/20230210-193140.jpg)![A later duel in Studio Beta](/eop-2023/20230210-193800.jpg)

Following a heroic effort to get the plot hung, focused, and programmed, the show opened as planned less than a week after the flood.  Everything went well for the first two performances, but just before the third, there was another unpleasant surprise.  During channel check, we discovered that one of the movers I was using for figure 8 effects had blown its lamp.  Furthermore, when we tried to swap it, we discovered that the blown lamp had welded itself to the socket.  With this fixture out of commission, we quickly reprogrammed the remaining one to move in a bigger pattern to compensate.

## The Original Design Rediscovered

![An Augment3d rendering of my original design](/eop-2023/eop-playhouse-rendering.png)

Two years later, I was finally able to recreate my original design in Augment3d, a visualization software built into Eos.  I imported my 3D model of the Playhouse, placed and focused the lights, and reprogrammed the cues to make the first and only record of what the Playhouse looked like just 2 hours before the flood.
