# RPI Players Technical Committee (TCOMM)

![The RPI Players lighting storage cage](/tcomm/cage.jpg)

The RPI Players Technical Committee (TCOMM) is appointed by the Technical Director each semester and is tasked with maintaining all of the Players' equipment, teaching new members how to use each department's equipment, ensuring safe operations in each department, purchasing new equipment, and before the flood on February 4, 2023, maintaining and operating the RPI Playhouse.  I was the lighting department chair for 4 semesters, beginning only months after the flood.

## Project 1: The Cage

My first priority as lighting chair was to get all of the usable equipment out of the playhouse and find a way to get rid of anything that was unsafe or beyond repair.  The immediate, and obvious, answer to the storage problem was a cage on an accessible floor of another campus building where some older equipment was already stored.  Over the next year, I reorganized this cage 3 times to make all of the usable equipment easy to find, easy to take out, and easy to keep track of.  The photo above was taken after the third and final reorganization.

Only a detailed story could even begin to describe what this cage looked like at the beginning.  Picture a dark, metal cage, where all of the overhead lights are blown out.  Now, imagine trying to open the door, but the door gets stuck on something before it can open enough for someone to walk in.  Once the area is cleared a bit and the door is finally open, imagine stepping into the cage only to trip over old set pieces.  Then, imagine pushing further in, only to brush up against exposed fiberglass insulation and trip over more clutter, all while it keeps getting darker.  No words can possibly describe what a hazard this cage was, and no pictures were taken, as any evidence of this situation would have created trouble.  1 year later, after months of my hard work, this cage was lit up, easy to walk in, and neatly organized and labeled.

## Project 2: Fixture Maintenance

If the cage situation wasn't already bad enough, the condition of all of the lighting equipment was not much better.  Numerous incidents had occurred over the first year of my time with the Players, including lights just not working, electrical connectors falling off, thick layers of dust burning off of fixtures while they were in use, and terrible optics that could not be fixed, as the lamp focus knob was stuck.  Then, in Fall 2023, during the load in for *She Kills Monsters*, sparks flew out the back of a light fixture due to a cable being miswired.  After this incident, I decided that I'd had enough of these problems, and it was my responsibility as department chair to repair this equipment before anyone got hurt.

The initial attempt at fixture maintenance involved wiping the outside of each fixture with a cloth and aligning the lamp.  This was short-lived, as even a simple cleaning was not enough to get the lamp focus knobs unstuck.  Additionally, the burning smell was still present on a large number of fixtures.  At this point, I decided that the only way to permanently fix these problems was to completely disassemble each fixture, thoroughly clean each part, replace broken parts, reassemble the fixture, and then align the lamp.  This process took 8 months to complete, and in total, I repaired over 80 lighting fixtures and over 50 cables to a point where all of this equipment was like new.  For more details, and some concerning photos, check out my documentation from this project.

## Project 3: Documentation & Training

To combat the ignorance that likely led to the equipment problems, I compiled manuals, datasheets, and assembly guides for every piece of equipment that had its documentation published.  I also wrote basic instructions for setting up the console while on the road, and a guide to being a lighting designer, inspired by my process from *She Kills Monsters*.  I also reformatted and updated the inventory spreadsheet and reorganized the gel storage to make things easier to find.

## Project 4: New Equipment

Procuring new equipment had been a high priority for me since my first show with the RPI Players, as almost all of the lighting fixtures were incandescent, and both lamps and gels had been getting more expensive each year.  Once the Playhouse flooded, this became an even higher priority, as setting up a dimming system on the road is far more difficult than using LED fixtures.  During my time as department chair, I purchased 3 LED fixtures and received a donation of 5 from EMPAC that were intended for use as eye-candy.
