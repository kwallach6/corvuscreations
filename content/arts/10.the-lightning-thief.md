# The Lightning Thief

![The cast of The Lightning Thief](/the-lightning-thief/tlt-cast.jpg)

The RPI Players produced *The Lightning Thief* in Spring 2024, and this was the first mainstage show since the RPI Playhouse flood that was produced in a single venue.  I was the master electrician for this show, and this was one of the most challenging shows I have worked on, mainly due to the nature of the venue and some last-minute staffing issues.

## The Venue

The Schacht Fine Arts Center at Russell Sage College is a beautiful mid-size proscenium theater, but in terms of lighting, it is a difficult venue to work with.  Most of the dimming and power transmission systems are ancient and, due to frequent staff and student turnover between productions, hard and soft patches change regularly.  After hanging our plot, it took three days to get all of the lights working properly before programming could even start, as our lighting team was inexperienced at reading paperwork.  In essence, this turned out to be a complicated puzzle of matching numbers between documents, checking cables, and editing the soft patch.  To control a fixture, one must identify it on the plot, match the channel number to the respective column on the channel hookup, match the DMX address on the channel hookup to the respective column on the instrument schedule, and then determine the circuit number and dimmer number (different from the address) from both the instrument schedule and a dimmer hookup.  Check out some of the paperwork from this show to see just how complicated this was!

## The Show Must Go On

![A dramatic lighting cue from The Lightning Thief](/the-lightning-thief/kronos.jpg)

Not only was the venue a puzzle to solve, but staffing the lighting department for this show was complicated.  Three weeks before tech, one of the lighting designers was fired after failing to produce any meaningful work and threatening a member of the crew.  The remaining lighting designer, however, was inexperienced and had requested a co-designer to learn from.  Days later, a replacement co-designer was hired, and over the next few weeks, a rough design was presented to me.

By the end of tech week, most of the rough design ideas had not been finalized.  I had finally gotten all of the lights to work and organized them into groups, but programming had not yet started.  At this point, the director stepped in to design the show herself, as she realized that there would otherwise be no lighting for the show.  As a result, this show became much more like a professional experience, where the director would explain her vision, choose colors, and describe effects, and I would translate that into each cue.  This process also went quickly, as I was able to get everything programmed in a matter of hours and then touch things up after each run.  While this showfile was still messy, it was much better than the one from *Heathers* a year prior.
