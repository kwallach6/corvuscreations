# She Kills Monsters

![skm-cast](/she-kills-monsters/skm-cast.jpg)

I was a lighting co-designer for the RPI Players' production of *She Kills Monsters* in Fall 2023.  This was the Players' second mainstage production on the road after the RPI Playhouse flooded on February 4, 2023.  As a result of the club's inexperience with traveling productions, this was a set-less show, requiring much more effort from the lighting department to set the scene.  This is one of the shows I am most proud of, as even with the two-venue schedule, it came out incredibly well!

## Design Process

After my first readthrough of the script, I compiled a list of general ideas and shared them with my co-designer, Thomas (Tommy) Schmitz, and to my surprise, he agreed with every one of them without question.  We then proceeded to script each cue and each look that we wanted to build, but only in general terms, as we could not make our lighting plot until the venue was finalized.  As *She Kills Monsters* revolves around the role-playing game, *Dungeons and Dragons*, our central theme with lighting was to distinguish between the drab, boring real world and the colorful, imaginative fantasy world.  To achieve this, we selected neutral colors to use in our front wash and downlights for the real world, and a three-color mix of orange, purple, and green to use in our sidelights for the fantasy world.  Most of our cues involved switching between worlds, with some color emphasis for specific monsters, but we also built an extremely dramatic reveal effect for the final boss.  Check out my initial ideas and some of the prototyping photos below!

## Venue #1: The Sanctuary for Independent Media

![She Kills Monsters in the Sanctuary for Independent Media](/she-kills-monsters/IMG-0991.jpg)

The Sanctuary for Independent Media in North Troy, NY, is a beautiful chapel-like building that serves emerging artists and whose staff fight for the freedom of expression.  It has a small stage and a basic lighting grid, so initially, the master electrician told us we could plan on hanging our lights on the grid using a ladder and harness, and proceed just as if the show were in the Playhouse.  Tommy and I drew our plot for this venue, thinking that this would be easy, however, during load in, the director decided that this raised stage was too small to be able to safely perform fight scenes.  As such, an area of the house floor was taped out to be the stage, and all of the seating was rotated 90 degrees to face it.

![The original lighting plot for the Sanctuary](/she-kills-monsters/original-sanctuary-plot.png)

### The Ladder Incident

With the flipped stage, Tommy and I quickly redrew our plot to have the fixtures placed in a line, front to back.  This seemed feasible at first, but proved to be much more difficult.  To hang a fixture, one would have to climb a 30-foot wooden extension ladder that could only be braced against a wooden truss on the ceiling.  Furthermore, the easiest and safest way to bring a fixture to the grid is to hoist it with a rope, which neither we nor the venue had available.  In a true engineering fashion, the technical director climbed the ladder unharnessed with a crowd of crew members bracing it for stability.  We then twisted a sheet of muslin into a long rope to use as a hoist.  This process was successful for clearing space and hanging the first fixture, however, with over 20 lights to hang, the technical director ruled this unsafe, especially after seeing the rope unravel during the hoisting process.

![The lighting plot for the flipped stage](/she-kills-monsters/flipped-sanctuary-plot.png)

### Saved by a Freelancer

Following the near-catastrophic ladder incident, the master electrician told us that there was now a good chance that there would not be lighting in this venue.  At the same time, an outside volunteer was packing up his equipment from a previous gig and overheard this discussion.  He introduced himself as a freelancer by the trade name "Nicky Lightz", who lends equipment to schools and community performance groups who need it.  After witnessing the ladder incident and learning that I worked for a friend of his at EMPAC, he offered to lend us everything that he was packing up free of charge.  Tommy and I quickly drew a new plot and got to work hanging our fixtures on Nick's trees.  In the process, I also had to program a custom fixture personality for the LEDs he gave us.  Thanks to "Nicky Lights", the show went on looking beautiful!

![The lighting plot for using the equipment from Nicky Lightz](/she-kills-monsters/nicky-lightz-plot.png)

## The Arts Center of the Capital Region

![She Kills Monsters in the Arts Center of the Capital Region](/she-kills-monsters/fairy.jpg)

The Arts Center of the Capital Region serves a similar purpose to the Sanctuary for Independent Media, however, it houses a small blackbox theater that is actually intended for live performances.  This venue was equipped with a set of Chauvet COLORADO LED fixtures that had a motorized zoom, making our design much easier to implement.  Additionally, it had a permanently installed ETC Sensor rack, making dimming for incandescent fixtures easy.  Thus, with all of these benefits, Tommy and I got away with making only one plot for this venue.

![The lighting plot for the Arts Center](/she-kills-monsters/accr-plot.png)

### Another Close Call

With no tall ladder or high ceiling to worry about, it seemed as if nothing could go wrong in this venue.  Unfortunately, we were met with another surprise during load in.  After finally figuring out how to allocate dimmers to the incandescent fixtures while leaving enough hot power for the LED fixtures, light hang proceeded as planned.  Channel check, however, resulted in a nasty surprise.  When one of the Source 4s for the front wash was tested, I heard a loud "pop" as a shower of sparks flew out the back of the fixture and the circuit breaker tripped.  This would prove to be the result of a miswired cable, and was the final straw leading to my [landmark equipment maintenance project](/arts/tcomm).
