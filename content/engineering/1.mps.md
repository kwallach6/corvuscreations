# Manufacturing Processes and Systems Lab

![The MPS RPIggy Bank team.](/mps/mps-team.jpg)

The Manufacturing Processes and Systems Lab course tasks students with designing and producing 300 units of a product while using a variety of processes, including plastic injection molding, deep drawing, vacuum forming, waterjet cutting, lasercutting, CNC machining, 3D printing, and robotic assembly.  Four teams spend the fall semester designing a product and its related tooling and fixtures, and two are selected to manufacture all 300 units in the spring semester, using and improving plans from the fall.

## MPS 1: Handheld Hockey Design

The first three weeks of the course asked my team to design a product with enough components to exercise most of the manufacturing processes available in the Manufacturing Innovation Learning Lab (MILL).  The initial concept was selected from a catalog of ideas pitched by the teaching team, and even the final products the team produced at the end of the spring semester still somewhat resemble this initial idea!

My role in this design process was the assembly line, specifically the automated workstations. From the beginning, I knew that every component except for the stickers, spring, and bubble top were feasible for assembly with the Universal Robots UR10e arms and either a Robotiq Hand-E or AirPick gripper.  This process sounded simple, picking parts and placing them into their correct locations, but when trying to "teach something with an IQ of 0", as my instructor describes it, things become much more complicated.  To complicate things further, some parts, such as the goal, were too large to be gripped by the Hand-E, and the handle needed to be screwed onto the bolt precisely.  My solution to this problem was finger extenders and a kitting fixture for the conveyor that brings parts around to each station.  I then tasked the team's designers with modeling this conveyor kit and the gripper extenders, while I simulated the assembly process in [Octopuz.](https://octopuz.com)

## MPS 2: RPIggy Bank Production

Following the fall semester presentations, I switched teams to help the RPIggy Bank designers with their assembly line design.  Initially, their assembly plan depended on palletizing every component in mass quantities, which would have consumed a large amount of space on the assembly tables and required excessive labor during preparation. To address this, our assembly line redesign consolidated operations into three main stations, the legs-base-back subassembly, the headless subassembly, and the final assembly.  Heads were to be prepared ahead of time due to the labor needed for circuit assembly.  I again focused on the automated stations for this project.

![rpiggy-bank](/mps/rpiggy-bank.jpg){.w-auto height="400"}

### Legs-Base-Back Subassembly

The legs-base-back process was the most difficult program I wrote for this project.  Each part needed to be gripped in a specific orientation, and there were so many fixtures needed that everything supporting the assembly process became an obstacle for the robot to maneuver around.  The programming process began with an Octopuz simulation, which determined that moving a pallet off the conveyor to the table was necessary to avoid collisions.  The rest of the simulation helped structure the robot code but ultimately did not add anything meaningful to the final program.  This program took 4 months to complete, and the process, from simulation to production run, is shown in the following videos!

### Headless Subassembly

The headless subassembly process places the arms onto the back half of the body.  With the experience I gained from the legs-base-back program, I was able to complete this equally difficult program in just three weeks!  The most challenging part of this program was pressing the arms onto their rings, as the robot could not exert enough force without impacting the arm, which is dangerous.  The solution was a pneumatic ram that my assembly manager and I devised and constructed in a week!  Check out videos of this process below!
